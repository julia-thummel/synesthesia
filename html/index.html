Synesthesia

noun:
psychology,
physiology
syn·es·the·sia
/sinəsˈTHēZHə/
A rare neurological phenomenon that causes a person’s senses to work together in ways different from most other humans.

First of all,

how does it work?

perception

People with synesthesia automatically and involuntarily perceive the world differently from others due to biological abnormlities in the wiring of their brains.

connection

Synesthesia occurs as a result of neural connections forming between areas of the brain that control the senses.

These areas are usually not connected, but these abnormal neural pathways allow for additional communication and result in a synesthetic response.

interesting. and now,

how many people have it?

Only a small percentage of the population has synesthesia - about 3% to be exact - although no one really knows if that number is accurate since synesthesia is usually self-diagnosed.

Often times synesthetes don’t talk about their condition because they are either unaware that others don’t perceive the world the same way, or they are embarassed to be different.

Though each synesthete may have a different trigger and personal response, they do tend to have one thing in common, in that most have a tendency to be creative.

and finally,

what kinds are there?

Synesthetic responses are categorized into two main groups: projective and associative. Projective synesthesia means that a person would respond to a stimulus by literally seeing something in their mind’s eye.

Associative synesthesia on the other hand means a person involuntarily creates a strong connection between the stimulus and another sense. A wide variety of associative synesthetic responses have been documented, but some are more commmon than others.
